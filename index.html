Prepare for the Assessment

Everything you need to know to ace both sections of the test.

Behavioral questions ask you to share real examples from your past experiences. These questions help us understand how you approach challenges, make decisions, and work with others.

We evaluate your responses based on the six core traits we're looking for: Innovation, Data-driven thinking, Curiosity, Results orientation, Communication, and Accountability.

We avoid brain teasers like "How many windows are in Manhattan?" because they don't reliably predict success. Instead, we want to hear real stories that demonstrate these traits.

Here are examples of questions you might be asked:
• Tell me about a time you faced a problem with multiple possible solutions. How did you decide what to do, and what was the outcome?
• Describe a time when you took a risk, made a mistake, or failed. What happened, and what did you learn from it?
• Share an example of when you took the lead on a project.
• What did you do to motivate a group or encourage collaboration on a team project?
• How have you used data to develop a strategy or make a decision?
When you're asked to describe something you've done, we're not looking for a quick answer—we want to hear a complete story. A strong response gives us insight into how you think, act, and problem-solve.

Your answer should include:
• Context – What was going on, and what was your role?
• Task – What were you trying to accomplish?
• Actions – What did you specifically do to move things forward?
• Outcome – How did it turn out? What did you learn?

We don't expect perfect answers or rehearsed scripts. The STAR method is simply a tool to help you organize your thoughts and tell a story that brings your strengths to life.

S = SITUATION
Start by setting the scene. What was going on? Where were you working (or studying or volunteering), and what were you trying to tackle? Give just enough background so we understand the big picture.

T = TASK
What was your goal? What were you responsible for? This helps us understand your role and what you were trying to achieve.

A = ACTION
Walk us through what you did. What steps did you take? How did you approach the challenge? Focus on your individual contributions—use "I" instead of "we."

R = RESULT
How did things turn out? Share what you accomplished and what you learned. If you have numbers or specific outcomes, even better.

Remember, not every story has to be a success. We know that failure is part of growth. What matters is what you learned and how you improved.
Think in stories
Each answer should have a beginning, middle, and end. What was the situation? What did you do? What happened next?

Be specific
Use real examples from your past—not generalizations. Give a detailed account of one situation for each question. Focus on what you did, and back it up with data or results if possible.

Balance detail with clarity
Try to be clear and concise, but include enough detail to tell a complete story. ~100 words per answer is the sweet spot.

Show range
Have a few examples ready that highlight different strengths, including times you've taken risks, succeeded, failed, and grown.

Demonstrate the traits we're looking for
Your answers should showcase Innovation, Data-driven thinking, Curiosity, Results orientation, Communication, and Accountability. The best answers naturally weave in multiple traits.

Keep it honest
We're not looking for perfection. We value authenticity, self-awareness, and a willingness to learn. Don't make things up—we do background checks.

We don't just want code that works—we want code that's efficient. Understanding complexity is essential because:

Time Complexity (Big O)
This tells us how your algorithm's runtime grows as input size increases. Know the difference between O(1), O(log n), O(n), O(n log n), O(n²), and O(2^n). A solution that works for 100 elements but crashes on 100,000 won't cut it.

Space Complexity
This measures how much additional memory your algorithm needs. Sometimes you can trade space for time (or vice versa). Understanding this trade-off is crucial.

What we expect:
• Analyze the complexity of your solution before coding
• Explain why you chose a particular approach
• Discuss trade-offs between different solutions
• Optimize when possible—brute force is just the starting point

Pro tip: In your written explanation, always mention the time and space complexity of your solution. This shows you understand the fundamentals, not just the syntax.
